##### What is glutathione - Asked Perplexity AI June 2024

Glutathione is a powerful antioxidant naturally produced in every cell of the human body.

Here are the key points about glutathione:

1. Composition: It is a tripeptide composed of three amino acids - cysteine, glycine, and glutamic acid.

1. Production: Glutathione is synthesized primarily in the liver and is involved in many bodily processes.

1. Functions: Glutathione plays critical roles in:

   * Detoxification of xenobiotic and endogenous compounds

   * Tissue building and repair

   * Making chemicals and proteins needed in the body

   * Supporting immune system function

   * DNA synthesis

   * Neutralizing free radicals

   * Regenerating vitamins C and E

   * Facilitating mercury elimination from the brain

1. Antioxidant properties: As a potent antioxidant, glutathione helps protect cells from oxidative damage and maintains redox homeostasis.

1. Health implications: Low levels of glutathione have been associated with various health conditions,  
 including  **liver disease, cancer, Parkinson's disease, and other neurodegenerative disorders.** 

1. Supplementation: Glutathione levels can be boosted through oral or intravenous supplementation,   
or by taking precursors that stimulate the body's natural production.

1. Measurement: Assessing glutathione status in cells is important for understanding oxidative stress and redox signaling.

1.  **Age-related decline:**  Glutathione levels tend to decrease with age, which may contribute to various age-related health issues.

While glutathione shows promise in various health applications, it's important to consult with a healthcare professional before starting any supplementation regimen.

##### Describe the top antioxidants in order of importance - Asked Perplexity AI June 2024

Based on the search results and scientific understanding, here are the top antioxidants for the human body, listed in approximate order of importance:

1.  **Glutathione: Often called the "master antioxidant,"**  glutathione is the most potent antioxidant produced by our bodies. It supports lung health, liver detoxification, immune function, and can reduce signs of aging skin.

1. Vitamin C: A powerful water-soluble antioxidant crucial for immune function. It can be found in fruits and vegetables, especially citrus fruits, berries, and red peppers.

1. Vitamin E: This fat-soluble antioxidant protects cell membranes and can be found in foods like nuts, seeds, and vegetable oils.

1. Coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10): Found in every cell of the body, CoQ10 protects cell membranes from free radical damage and is essential for energy production.

1. Selenium: This mineral is required for the production of important antioxidant enzymes in the body. Brazil nuts are an excellent source of selenium.

1. Carotenoids (including beta-carotene and lycopene): Found in orange and red fruits and vegetables, these antioxidants have been linked to various health benefits, including reduced risk of certain cancers.

1. Flavonoids: These plant compounds are found in a variety of fruits, vegetables, and beverages like tea and red wine. They have been associated with preventing metabolic diseases and certain cancers.

1. Anthocyanins: Found in blue and purple fruits like blueberries and blackberries, these antioxidants have been linked to heart health and reduced inflammation

Vitamin D receptors are expressed in many organs and tissues, which suggests that vitamin D (VD) affects physiological functions beyond its role in maintaining bone health. Deficiency or inadequacy of 25(OH)VD is widespread globally. Population studies demonstrate that a positive association exists between a high incidence of VD deficiency and a high incidence of chronic diseases, including dementia, diabetes, and heart disease. However, many subjects have difficulty achieving the required circulating levels of 25(OH)VD even after high-dose VD supplementation, and randomized controlled clinical trials have reported limited therapeutic success post-VD supplementation. Thus, there is a discordance between the benefits of VD supplementation and the prevention of chronic diseases in those with VD deficiency. Why this dissociation exists is currently under debate and is of significant public interest. 

This review discusses the downregulation of VD-metabolizing genes needed to convert consumed VD into 25(OH)VD to enable its metabolic action exhibited by subjects with metabolic syndrome, obesity, and other chronic diseases. Research findings indicate a positive correlation between the levels of 25(OH)VD and glutathione (GSH) in both healthy and diabetic individuals. 

Cell culture and animal experiments reveal a novel mechanism through which the status of GSH can positively impact the expression of VD metabolism genes. 

This review highlights that for better success, VD deficiency needs to be corrected at multiple levels: 

* (i) VD supplements and/or VD-rich foods need to be consumed to provide adequate VD, and 

* (ii) the body needs to be able to upregulate VD-metabolizing genes to convert VD into 25(OH)VD and then to 1,25(OH)2VD to enhance its metabolic action. 

This review outlines the association between 25(OH)VD deficiency/inadequacy and decreased GSH levels, highlighting the positive impact of combined VD+LC supplementation on upregulating GSH, VD-metabolizing genes, and VDR. These effects have the potential to enhance 25(OH)VD levels and its therapeutic efficacy.

#### Have glutathione levels decreased recently (spolier: YES) - asked Chat-GPT 4.o June 2024

There is some evidence suggesting that glutathione levels have decreased in certain contexts over recent decades. Several factors contribute to these changes, including increased exposure to environmental pollutants, dietary shifts, and the widespread use of certain medications.

1.** Environmental Pollutants **: The rise in environmental pollutants, such as heavy metals, pesticides, and industrial chemicals, has been linked to oxidative stress, which depletes glutathione levels. This is because glutathione is a primary antioxidant that neutralizes reactive oxygen species (ROS) induced by these pollutants ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/journal/molecules/special_issues/glutathione_biochemistry)) ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/1420-3049/28/3/1447)).

1.** Dietary Changes **: Modern diets, which are often lower in fruits and vegetables and higher in processed foods, can reduce the intake of glutathione precursors and other antioxidants. Nutrient deficiencies, particularly in vitamins and minerals that support glutathione synthesis, may also play a role in the decline of glutathione levels ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/journal/molecules/special_issues/glutathione_biochemistry)).

1.** Medication Use **: Certain medications, including acetaminophen (paracetamol), can deplete glutathione levels. Acetaminophen, when metabolized in the liver, can form toxic intermediates that require glutathione for detoxification, thereby reducing its availability ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/15/24/5104)).

1.** Aging **: Natural aging processes are associated with a decline in glutathione levels. This is due to a decrease in the efficiency of the body's natural antioxidant systems and increased oxidative stress over time ([MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/1420-3049/28/3/1447)).

Overall, while it is challenging to pinpoint a precise global trend, these factors collectively suggest that glutathione levels might have decreased in recent decades due to increased oxidative stress from environmental, dietary, and pharmaceutical sources.
